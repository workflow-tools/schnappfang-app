# Schnappfang — Species Priority Justification

> Companion to `bayern-species-smoketest.csv`. Explains why these 18 species
> were chosen as the priority list for the smoke test species info database.
>
> **Drafted:** 2026-04-11. **Pivoted:** 2026-04-12. **Author:** Ryan Hill.

---

## Pivot Notice (2026-04-12)

The Schnappfang MVP pivoted from a **regulation-verdict card** to a
**convenience-first composable catch card**. The insight: by the time an
angler uses Schnappfang, the keep/release decision is already behind them.
A retrospective verdict answers a question nobody is asking while inheriting
€5,000-fine liability. The card is now a fun, shareable trophy card with
optional info blocks — not a compliance tool.

**What changed in this file:**

- **Criterion B (regulation archetype coverage) is no longer load-bearing.**
  It was the original reason the list needed to be exactly 18 species; without
  it the list could in principle shrink. However, all 18 species independently
  justify themselves on the remaining criteria (photo-likelihood, geography,
  fun-fact/recipe richness), so the list stays at 18.
- **Criterion D was renamed** from "Regulation-interesting factor" to
  "Story-interesting factor." The same species that had surprising regulation
  stories (Äsche smells like thyme, Huchen is the Donaulachs, Aal migrates
  5,000 km) have even more compelling fun facts for card content.
- **The companion CSV changed** from `bayern-regulations-smoketest.csv` (a
  regulation workbook) to `bayern-species-smoketest.csv` (a species info
  database with fun facts, recipes, moon/solunar data, and photo notes).
- **The verdict language was removed** throughout. No more
  `entnahme_erlaubt`, `zuruecksetzen`, `ganzjaehrig_geschuetzt` archetypes.
- **Regulation appears on cards only as** an informational season mention
  and a link to official sources. Not a verdict.

## 1. How the list was constructed

The priority list is optimised against four simultaneous criteria. A species
earns its slot by scoring on at least two of them; species that would score
on only one were cut.

### Criterion A — Photo-likelihood

The smoke test depends on 12–15 *real* photos from Oberpfalz angler contacts
(§6 of the smoke test plan). The list must skew toward species that actually
end up in an angler's camera roll. This means:

- Trophy predators that get photographed on principle (Hecht, Zander, Wels,
  Huchen)
- Photogenic salmonids (Bachforelle, Regenbogenforelle, Seeforelle, Saibling,
  Äsche)
- Iconic coarse species that anglers display (big Karpfen, big Brachse,
  golden Schleie)
- Ubiquitous bycatch that shows up as "first catch of the day" snapshots
  (Barsch, Rotauge, Aitel)

Tiny stream fish (Elritze, Moderlieschen, Bachneunauge) almost never appear
in angler photos and are excluded from the priority list.

### Criterion B — Fun-info richness (post-pivot)

*Previously: "Regulation archetype coverage." Replaced 2026-04-12.*

The card is now a composable layout with optional info blocks. Each species
must have enough interesting content to fill those blocks and make the card
worth sharing. The info blocks are:

- **Species fun fact** — every species must have at least one genuinely
  surprising or delightful fact in German. "The fish is common" is not a
  fun fact; "freshly caught Äsche smells like thyme" is.
- **German name origin** — etymology of the common name. This is the
  Germany-specific fun content that replaces regulation data as the
  localization moat.
- **Recipe pair** — camp + home variant. Species without either recipe
  (Huchen and Nase, both rare and/or protected) are noted as gaps.
- **Moon/solunar relevance** — a sentence on whether moon phase matters
  for this species. Anglers care about this.
- **Photo notes** — guidance for card template selection and cropping.
  Long-body fish (Wels, Aal) stress-test the template differently than
  tall-body fish (Brachse).

18/18 species have fun facts and name origins populated. 16/18 have both
recipe variants (Huchen and Nase are the two gaps — both are too rare/
protected for cooking to be appropriate on a card).

### Criterion C — Oberpfalz geography

The founder lives in Vilseck. Photos come from local contacts. The region's
water types must be represented:

- **Donau drainage rivers** (Naab, Vils, Pfreimd) — Hecht, Zander, Wels,
  Aal, Aitel/Döbel, Nase, occasional Huchen
- **Forellenbäche** — Bachforelle and Äsche
- **Teichwirtschaft** — Karpfen (Oberpfälzer Karpfen is a protected
  indication of origin), Schleie, Brachse, Rotauge
- **Alpine foothills / lakes** (~2 h south) — Saibling, Seeforelle, Renke

All four habitats are covered.

### Criterion D — Story-interesting factor (post-pivot)

*Previously: "Regulation-interesting factor." Reframed 2026-04-12.*

Species included specifically because they have a story that makes the
card worth sharing — not a regulation verdict, but a genuine "oh,
I didn't know that" moment:

- **Äsche** — smells like thyme when freshly caught; the Latin name
  *Thymallus* encodes this. The segelartige Rückenflosse is visually
  unmistakable. Status varies locally — the card links to regulations
  rather than ruling on them.
- **Huchen** — the Donaulachs, one of Europe's rarest freshwater fish.
  Bayern holds a significant chunk of the global population. A Huchen
  photo on a card is an event.
- **Aal** — migrates 5,000+ km to the Sargasso Sea to spawn and nobody
  has ever observed it happen. One of biology's great unsolved mysteries.
  IUCN critically endangered. A card with this fact will get shared.
- **Nase** — once among Bayern's most abundant river fish, now deeply
  depleted with active reintroduction programmes. The conservation story
  is compelling and educational.
- **Karpfen** — "Oberpfälzer Karpfen" is a protected indication of
  origin. Christmas/Silvester carp is a regional food tradition. The
  cultural story is card-worthy for Oberpfalz anglers.
- **Schleie** — the "Doktorfisch" whose slime was used medicinally in
  the Middle Ages. Delightful trivia.

## 2. The 18 species, tier by tier

Each entry names the species in German and Latin, gives the one-line reason
it is on the list, and flags which criteria carry it.

### Tier 1 — Core predators (4 species)

1. **Hecht** — *Esox lucius*
   Bayern's iconic predator. Every angler photo set has Hechte. The
   "Wasserwolf" nickname and half-body-length prey fact are strong card
   content. Long-body format stress-tests the card template.
   *Carries on:* A (photo), B (fun fact, recipe, photo notes), C (Donau).

2. **Zander** — *Sander lucioperca*
   Donau-classic. Germany's most prized freshwater table fish in fine
   dining. The "largest fangs of any native freshwater fish" fact is
   card-worthy. Glowing eyes in flash photos = useful photo note.
   *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact, recipe, photo notes), C.

3. **Wels** — *Silurus glanis*
   Europe's largest freshwater fish. Donau trophy. 2.5 m+ specimens
   documented near Passau. Also called "Waller" in Bayern — the regional
   name is card content. Extreme long-body aspect ratio tests template.
   *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact, recipe, photo notes), C.

4. **Huchen** — *Hucho hucho*
   The Donaulachs. One of Europe's rarest freshwater fish. Bayern holds
   a significant share of the global population. A Huchen photo on a
   card is a trophy event. No recipe provided (too rare/restricted).
   *Carries on:* A (rare but high-value), C, D (story).

### Tier 2 — Salmonids (5 species)

5. **Bachforelle** — *Salmo trutta fario*
   Native Forellenbach trout. "No two Bachforellen look the same" — the
   fingerprint-pattern fact is strong. Forelle Müllerin is a Bavarian
   cooking institution. Photo note: wet stone or in-water shots.
   *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact, recipe, photo notes), C (Forellenbach).

6. **Regenbogenforelle** — *Oncorhynchus mykiss*
   Most-stocked salmonid in Germany. "A Neozoon that became a regular"
   since the 1880s. Contrast with Bachforelle — same template shape,
   different story. Rainbow stripe rarely shows in photos (useful note).
   *Carries on:* A, B, C.

7. **Seeforelle** — *Salmo trutta lacustris*
   Alpine lake predator. "The shark of the Voralpenseen" at 10+ kg.
   Autumn spawning migration is a Naturschauspiel. Mountain-backdrop
   photos are strong card material.
   *Carries on:* A, B, C (Alpine lakes), D (story).

8. **Saibling** — *Salvelinus umbla*
   "Perhaps the most beautiful native freshwater fish" — the orange
   belly is the photo motif. Photo note: photograph wet, colours fade
   fast. Lives in Bayern's deepest, coldest lakes.
   *Carries on:* A, B (photo notes, fun fact), C.

9. **Äsche** — *Thymallus thymallus*
   Smells like thyme when freshly caught. The sail-like dorsal fin is
   visually unmistakable. Status varies locally — card links to
   regulations rather than ruling. Strongest "I didn't know that" fact.
   *Carries on:* A, B, C, D (the story-richest species on the list).

### Tier 3 — Cyprinid / coarse / stillwater (5 species)

10. **Karpfen** — *Cyprinus carpio*
    Oberpfälzer Karpfen = protected indication of origin. Christmas/
    Silvester tradition. Oberpfalz karpfenteich as card backdrop =
    regional identity. Recipe: "Karpfen gebacken mit Biersauce."
    *Carries on:* A (very high), B, C (Teichwirtschaft), D (cultural).

11. **Schleie** — *Tinca tinca*
    The "Doktorfisch" — medieval medicinal slime. Golden-green shimmer
    fades fast; photo note says shoot immediately. Mild, buttery flavour.
    *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact, photo notes), C.

12. **Brachse / Brasse** — *Abramis brama*
    "Feeding brachse plough the lakebed and leave visible feeding
    craters." Big slab shape = Hochformat may suit better than Querformat.
    Recipe solves the Grätenproblem via Fischfrikadelle.
    *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact, recipe, photo notes), C.

13. **Rotauge / Plötze** — *Rutilus rutilus*
    Bayern's most common fish. A 40 cm Rotauge is a genuine trophy among
    coarse anglers. Red eyes are the namesake and the photo motif. Rarely
    photographed = strong credibility signal if present.
    *Carries on:* B (credibility), C.

14. **Aitel / Döbel** — *Squalius cephalus*
    "Eats cherries, cheese, and even mice that fall into the water."
    The Allesfresser fact is delightful card content. Naab/Vils backdrop
    = regional authenticity.
    *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact), C (Oberpfalz-local).

### Tier 4 — Perch, migrants, and conservation stories (4 species)

15. **Flussbarsch** — *Perca fluviatilis*
    Most popular beginner target in Bayern. "Stripes like fingerprints."
    A 40 cm Barsch is a "Kapitaler." Broad target audience. Recipe:
    Barschfilet in Bierteig.
    *Carries on:* A, B (fun fact, recipe), C.

16. **Aal** — *Anguilla anguilla*
    5,000 km migration to the Sargasso Sea, never observed spawning.
    "One of biology's great unsolved mysteries." IUCN critically
    endangered. Snake shape polarises but stands out on the card.
    *Carries on:* B (story, photo notes), D (conservation story).

17. **Renke / Felchen** — *Coregonus spp.*
    THE Speisefisch of the Voralpenseen. "Renke Müllerin am Starnberger
    See ist ein Kulturgut." Regional name varies (Renke / Felchen /
    Maräne). Alpine lake backdrop. Rules vary by lake — card links out.
    *Carries on:* B (cultural), C, D.

18. **Nase** — *Chondrostoma nasus*
    Once among Bayern's most abundant river fish, now deeply depleted.
    Active reintroduction programmes. The nasal overbite is the namesake.
    No recipe provided (conservation species). A card featuring a Nase
    tells a conservation story.
    *Carries on:* C, D (conservation story).

## 3. Coverage gaps and known limitations

- **Recipes:** 16 of 18 species have camp + home recipe pairs. Huchen and
  Nase are intentionally left blank — Huchen because it is too rare and
  restricted for cooking to be appropriate on a card, Nase because it is
  a conservation species and promoting a recipe would be tone-deaf.
- **Moon/solunar data:** All 18 rows have a note, but the data is angler
  folklore, not science. The card should present it as "Anglers say…" rather
  than as fact.
- **Catch season hints:** These are informational descriptions of when the
  species is typically active, NOT regulation verdicts. The card links to
  `gesetze-bayern.de` for official rules. Some species (Huchen, Äsche, Nase,
  Renke) have hints that say "check locally" because their status is
  genuinely variable.
- **Photo sourcing:** Huchen and Nase are the hardest to source from personal
  contacts. Royalty-free fallback is noted in the photo_notes column.

## 4. What might be added in a v0.1 expansion

Species not in the priority list but worth considering for the full species
info database:

- **Barbe** (*Barbus barbus*) — river cyprinid, strong visual identity
- **Rutte / Quappe** (*Lota lota*) — winter-active, unusual lifecycle story
- **Karausche** (*Carassius carassius*) — rare, stillwater, can survive
  being frozen into ice (excellent fun fact)
- **Giebel** (*Carassius gibelio*) — invasive, reproduces by gynogenesis
  (another excellent fun fact)
- Small stream species (Elritze, Moderlieschen, Schmerle, Groppe, etc.) —
  these rarely get photographed but could appear in v0.1 as "did you know?"
  species-fact cards without a catch photo.
